# project-proposal-structure

WHEN TO USE: Loaded into the Package Drafter system prompt for the `project_proposal` material, as a fallback structure when the opportunity's own URL fetch did not surface a prescribed format (which is common for smaller foundations and state arts councils, and is the typical case for ~40% of matches). If the opportunity HAS a prescribed format — Creative Capital's handbook, the NEA Visual Arts discipline instructions, a foundation's own RFP — the drafter follows that prescription instead. This file supplies the generic shape plus worked examples in the two dominant registers (Creative-Capital-style project grant, Guggenheim-style research fellowship).

---

## 1. The six-beat structure

Every strong grant proposal at the foundation / fellowship / residency level — across Creative Capital, Guggenheim, MacDowell's optional project-context field, NEA Grants for Arts Projects Visual Arts, Pollock-Krasner, Joan Mitchell Fellowship's 300-word statements, and USA Fellowship — lands the same six beats, in the same order, with length calibrated to the word budget. The beats are not section headings unless the program requests headings. They are the load-bearing joints of the prose.

1. **One-line thesis.** What is the project, in plain English, no jargon, one sentence. The panelist reads this and decides whether to read paragraph two.
2. **Context.** What existing body of work this extends; what conversation it enters. Two to four sentences, lineage-grounded, named precedents where load-bearing.
3. **Method.** Concrete materials, locations, procedure, and timeline in *months* — not in "phases." This is the beat that fails most applications: "Phase one: research. Phase two: photography. Phase three: editing." is a non-method. A method names locations (the three rivers, not "rural waterways"), tools (8x10 Deardorff, not "large format camera"), and calendar (months 1–3: scouting trips to Kennebec and Androscoggin; months 4–9: primary shooting at thaw and freeze events; months 10–12: contact-printing and sequencing).
4. **Deliverables.** What the funder gets at the end — a count, a format, a scale, a venue if known. "Thirty 40x50-inch archival pigment prints; a self-published monograph with TIS Books in a 1,500-copy first run; a solo exhibition at Dowling Walsh confirmed for fall 2025." Vagueness here reads as absence of plan.
5. **Budget frame.** Included only when asked. Creative Capital and NEA require itemized budgets; MacDowell and Yaddo do not; Pollock-Krasner asks the applicant to state the purpose and amount in the cover letter ([Pollock-Krasner How to Apply](https://www.pkf.org/apply/how-to-apply/)). When included, the budget frame is one paragraph, tied to the deliverables — each major line item maps to a named deliverable.
6. **Why now, why you.** One sentence each. Why the project is urgent now (a closing window, a site that will disappear, a body of work that has reached the point of needing support to complete). Why you — one concrete credential or stake, not a summary of the CV.

Total target: 400–600 words if the program specifies nothing. Scale down for 300-word statement prompts (Joan Mitchell) by compressing beats 2, 5, and 6 to one sentence each. Scale up to 1,000 words (Guggenheim Statement of Plans) by expanding beat 3 (method) with more procedural detail, not by adding beats.

## 2. Evaluation-criteria overlay

Creative Capital names three criteria — *artistic strength, project feasibility, timing of the artist's career* — with artistic innovation "most heavily weighted" ([Creative Capital Grant Application](https://creative-capital.org/creative-capital-award/award-application/)). NEA weights *artistic excellence and artistic merit equally*, by statutory requirement ([NEA Review Criteria](https://www.arts.gov/grants/grants-for-arts-projects/review-criteria)). MacDowell's sole criterion is "artistic excellence" ([MacDowell Application Guidelines](https://www.macdowell.org/apply/application-guidelines)). These framings matter: MacDowell's panel scores the work samples, not the proposal — so a MacDowell project-context field should be *brief* and let the work carry the load. Creative Capital's panel weights the proposal heavily — so Creative Capital demands more method, more deliverables, more feasibility evidence. A drafter who uses the same proposal text for both is bringing the wrong tool.

Two practical rules follow:

- **Feasibility = method + budget + deliverables.** A proposal that names three rivers, 24 months, 30 prints, a confirmed gallery, and a named publisher *reads feasible*. A proposal that names "the American West," "approximately two years," and "a body of work" does not.
- **Innovation ≠ novelty claim.** Do not write "this project is innovative because..." Innovation is demonstrated by the method and the lineage, not asserted. If the drafter must name a point of innovation, it should be material (a cameraless process, a site access no previous photographer has had) or structural (a serial return over 10 years that no prior work has attempted at this scale), never thematic.

## 3. Worked example — Creative-Capital-register project proposal (550 words)

### *River Ice: A Decadal Return to Three Maine Rivers* (Knopf)

> *River Ice* is a ten-year project to photograph the annual freeze and breakup of three Maine rivers — the Kennebec, Androscoggin, and Penobscot — from fixed vantage points, producing a visual record of climate-driven change in the northeastern watershed.
>
> The project extends a decade of project-based landscape practice that sits in the New Topographics lineage (Shore, Baltz) and the durational-return structure of Sugimoto's *Seascapes* ([Fraenkel Gallery](https://fraenkelgallery.com/portfolios/hiroshi-sugimoto-seascapes)). It enters a current conversation in photography-as-climate-witness visible in the last two Guggenheim photography cohorts (Matthew Brandt's Lakes and Reservoirs, Tarrah Krajnak's re-staged landscape) and in Richard Misrach's *Desert Cantos* as the long-form precedent ([Fraenkel Gallery](https://fraenkelgallery.com/portfolios/richard-misrach-desert-cantos)). No comparable serial project exists for the Gulf-of-Maine watershed.
>
> Method: three fixed vantage points per river, identified in a scouting season (months 1–3); primary shooting at each thaw (March–April) and freeze (November–December) across years 1–3 of the grant period, in 4x5 color negative on a Deardorff 45 field camera, with a stationary tripod reference and GPS-logged vantage coordinates. Processing: C-41 in-house; drum-scanned at 4,000 ppi; archived redundantly on LTO-8 and cloud. Printing: 40x50-inch archival pigment on Hahnemühle Photo Rag at a collaborating master printer (months 20–24). A second wave of shooting extends years 4–10 and is not part of this grant cycle.
>
> Deliverables over the 24-month grant period: 30 finished prints (10 per river); a sequenced book dummy for *River Ice, Vol. I* with TIS Books (verbal agreement in place); a solo exhibition at Dowling Walsh Gallery, Rockland (confirmed for October 2025, 36 running feet); and a public artist-talk at the Portland Museum of Art (in discussion).
>
> Budget frame: of the $50,000 award, $18,000 covers field travel and lodging for six shooting trips; $12,000 covers film, processing, and drum-scanning; $14,000 covers print production at exhibition scale; $6,000 covers the book dummy development. No general-support draw; no studio rent.
>
> Why now: the Kennebec ice season has shortened by an average of 13 days since 1970 ([USGS: Lake and River Ice](https://www.usgs.gov/special-topics/water-science-school/science/lake-and-river-ice)), and the three-vantage protocol must begin in the next eligible freeze to establish a baseline before the 2030s. Why Knopf: a ten-year prior body (*Kennebec River Book*, 2014; *Frozen Harbor*, 2019) establishes the baseline vantages, the access, and the working weather competence the project requires.

**What to borrow from this example.** Numbers throughout. Named locations, tools, papers, venues. Deliverables that the Rubric Matcher can verify. A budget frame tied to deliverables line-by-line. Why-now grounded in a cited data point, not in a rhetorical flourish.

## 4. Worked example — Guggenheim-register statement of plans (compressed, 380 words)

### Opening paragraph, to illustrate the register shift

> The proposed Fellowship year supports completion of *River Ice*, a project already underway on three Maine rivers. The project photographs the annual freeze and breakup of the Kennebec, Androscoggin, and Penobscot from fixed vantage points in 4x5 color negative, printed at exhibition scale. The first two seasons (2022–2023, 2023–2024) are complete; the Fellowship supports the 2025–2026 season and the final production and sequencing of a first monograph.
>
> The work extends a durational landscape practice rooted in the New Topographics tradition and in the long-form structure of Misrach's *Desert Cantos*. It is in conversation with the Foundation's recent photography cohorts — Matthew Brandt's indexical-material practice, Tarrah Krajnak's re-staged canonical landscape — as members of a photographic response to climate that the Foundation's advisers appear to be reading closely. The project does not claim that lineage as settled membership; it argues that the Maine-watershed subject fills a gap the cohort has not yet covered.

**Register shift from the Creative Capital version.** The Guggenheim statement leans on *positioning within the foundation's own recent cohort* — the Fellowship is a career-stage prize, not a project grant, so the panel is weighing the artist's trajectory and conversation more than the project's deliverables. Method and deliverables still appear but at lower resolution; cohort-fit appears at higher resolution.

## 5. Common failure modes to avoid

- **Passive-voice method.** "Photographs will be made" hides the artist. Use the artist as subject: "Knopf makes the photographs on..."
- **Vague timeline.** "Phase one, two, three" with no months. Use actual months mapped to actual seasons.
- **Deliverables-as-aspiration.** "A body of work will emerge" is not a deliverable. "Thirty prints by month 24, exhibited at..." is.
- **Budget-as-wish-list.** A budget that does not map to deliverables reads as unplanned. Map every line.
- **Unsupported "innovation" claim.** Do not announce innovation; demonstrate it through method.
- **Missing "why this institution."** Every proposal should name — even if only in one clause — why the application is addressed to *this* funder rather than to a generic funder. Why-this-institution logic lives in `juror-reading.md` and should carry across into the proposal.
