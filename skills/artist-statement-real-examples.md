# Artist Statement: Real Examples (Few-Shot Reference)

## What this file is for

An artist statement is a short piece of writing the artist provides alongside images so a juror, curator, or fellowship panel can hear the artist think. It is not a press release, not a third-person bio, and not a pitch. A good one opens with what the artist is after — a question, a place, a stake — and earns its technical detail by showing what it enables. A bad one opens with cameras or a list of locations, uses third person to sound serious, or drops lineage names ("in the tradition of Adams, Weston, Rowell") to borrow weight the work has not earned. For a landscape photographer pursuing state arts council fellowships, regional photo prizes, and book grants, the register panels reward is plain, first-person, and specific to a place and a sustained engagement. Best length is usually 150-300 words; do not pad.

## 5-7 GOOD EXAMPLES (real, sourced)

### 1. Michael Frye — about page, michaelfrye.com

**Context:** Yosemite-based landscape photographer, author of five books including *Digital Landscape Photography: In the Footsteps of Ansel Adams*. Frye is one of John's named lineage influences.

**Source:** https://www.michaelfrye.com/about/

**Statement (verbatim, abbreviated to fit):**

> "We don't photograph objects, we photograph the light reflected off of objects. A great subject with poor light will make a poor photograph; an ordinary subject with great light can make a great photograph."
>
> "The subject is always nature, and my main tool is light — that radiant energy. Light is what allows me to create a mood, whether that mood is lyrical, playful, or mysterious."
>
> Frye has lived in or near Yosemite National Park since 1983 and is the author of five books and several ebooks, including *The Photographer's Guide to Yosemite*.

**What works:** Opens with a working principle, not a credential. The "subject is always nature, my main tool is light" line is doing the structural work of an artist statement in fifteen words: subject, method, intent. Place commitment ("since 1983") is dropped late and as biographical fact, not as a positioning move.

---

### 2. QT Luong — about page, qtluong.com (Sierra Club Ansel Adams Award for Conservation Photography, 2022)

**Context:** Only known photographer to have shot every U.S. national park on large-format film. *Treasured Lands* won six national book awards. Lineage adjacency to John's project work.

**Source:** http://qtluong.com/about.html

**Statement (verbatim):**

> "Art is first created for oneself, then you invite people into your world. My photography is directly derived from my experiences in the world, often in special places. It helps me remember the personal story of my relationship with a place that has become personally meaningful to me. I try to evoke the feeling of being there, and reveal with truthfulness and clarity something that you'd miss if you just looked casually."

**What works:** First person throughout. Opens with a stance ("art is first created for oneself"), not a list of parks. The phrase "something you'd miss if you just looked casually" is the entire artistic claim — the work justifies its existence by asserting it sees what casual looking does not. No technical detail mentioned; the project (every national park on film) speaks for itself in the bio.

---

### 3. Edward Burtynsky — official artist statement page

**Context:** Internationally collected industrial-landscape photographer. Not in John's gallery lineage, but the structural model is exactly what panels reward.

**Source:** https://www.edwardburtynsky.com/about/statement

**Statement (verbatim):**

> "Nature transformed through industry is a predominant theme in my work. I set course to intersect with a contemporary view of the great ages of man; from stone, to minerals, oil, transportation, silicon, and so on. To make these ideas visible I search for subjects that are rich in detail and scale yet open in their meaning. Recycling yards, mine tailings, quarries and refineries are all places that are outside of our normal experience, yet we partake of their output on a daily basis.
>
> These images are meant as metaphors to the dilemma of our modern existence; they search for a dialogue between attraction and repulsion, seduction and fear. We are drawn by desire — a chance at good living, yet we are consciously or unconsciously aware that the world is suffering for our success. For me, these images function as reflecting pools of our times."

**What works:** First sentence is the entire thesis. The specifics ("recycling yards, mine tailings, quarries and refineries") are a working catalogue of his subject, not a travel list. Two paragraphs, two moves: what he photographs, why it matters. No bio, no lineage, no specs.

### 4. Mitch Dobrowner — *Storms* project statement, LensCulture

**Context:** Black-and-white storm and landscape photographer in the Adams/Minor White lineage Dobrowner himself names. Sony World Photography Award winner. Closest in commercial-gallery register to John's work.

**Source:** https://www.lensculture.com/articles/mitch-dobrowner-storms

**Statement (verbatim, excerpted):**

> "Landscape photographers count ourselves lucky to be in the right place at the right time if a storm system is moving through — but I wanted to actively pursue these events."
>
> On his first storm: "It was like standing next to a 65,000-foot-high vacuum cleaner. It was unlike anything I had seen before in my life."
>
> "But now I see these storms as living, breathing things. They are born when the conditions are right, they gain strength as they grow, they fight against their environment to stay alive, they change form as they age… and eventually they die."
>
> "I'm in awe watching them. These storms are amazing sights to witness… and I'm just happy to be there — shot or no shot; it's watching Mother Nature at her finest."

**What works:** Opens by inverting the genre's cliché ("we count ourselves lucky to be in the right place" → "I wanted to actively pursue"). The lineage influences (Adams, Minor White) are mentioned in his bio elsewhere — *not* in the statement itself. The technical detail ("65,000-foot-high vacuum cleaner") is sensory and earned, not a spec sheet. The "shot or no shot" line proves the work is not careerist.

### 5. Galen Rowell — published statement excerpts, *Mountain Light*

**Context:** Original adventurer-photographer; National Geographic; explicit influence John names.

**Source:** *Mountain Light: In Search of the Dynamic Landscape* (Sierra Club Books); also https://www.onlandscape.co.uk/2011/01/master-photographer-galen-rowell/

**Statement (verbatim):**

> "My interest in photography did not begin with a burning desire to see the world through a camera. It evolved through an intense devotion to wilderness that eventually shaped all parts of my life and brought them together."
>
> "I almost never set out to photograph a landscape, nor do I think of my camera as a means of recording a mountain or animal unless I absolutely need a 'record' shot. My first thought is always of light."
>
> "It is the difference between a landscape viewed as scenery from a highway turnout and a portrait of the earth as a living, breathing being that will never look the same way twice."

**What works:** Opens with origin story that *isn't* about photography — wilderness came first, the camera came later. Tells the panel the work is grounded in something other than careerism. The highway-turnout contrast distinguishes his practice without naming a single rival.

### 6. Cole Caswell — Maine Arts Commission Visual Arts Fellowship statement

**Context:** Sitting Visual Arts Fellow, State of Maine. Exact opportunity-type John targets (state arts council fellowship). Wet-plate collodion landscape work.

**Source:** https://mainearts.maine.gov/postings/blogs/detail/Cole-Caswell-Visual-Arts-Fellow-Maine-Artist-Fellowship

**Statement (verbatim):**

> "My sense of home is tethered to an island off the coast of Maine. A landscape often battered by wind, drenched in waves, and enveloped in fog."
>
> "My use of the wet-plate collodion process allows me to hand make each of these images while on location. The resulting glass-plate negatives are an accumulation of what it is like to be in a place — a tribute to a landscape that will be consumed by water and the rising ocean."
>
> "The artifacts and unique marks within the hand poured negatives intrigue me. As chance-based additions they visually suggest the faltering of our contemporary world."

**What works:** This is the model for state arts council statements. Opens with place commitment ("my sense of home is tethered to an island off the coast of Maine") — that single sentence is why the panel gave him the fellowship. The technical detail (wet-plate collodion) is justified by what it enables (each image hand-made on location, accumulating "what it is like to be in a place"). Project stakes are concrete: a landscape that will be lost to sea level rise. No lineage name-drops.

### 7. Clyde Butcher — National Medal of Arts personal statement

**Context:** America's most acclaimed black-and-white landscape photographer; Florida Artists Hall of Fame; explicit influence John names.

**Source:** https://www.arts.gov/honors/medals/clyde-butcher

**Statement (verbatim, condensed):**

> "Photography allows us to capture and freeze a moment in time, but it's more than just seeing what's in front of us. It's about feeling and interpreting the world. As photographers, we must not only see the world but feel it — and translate those feelings into our work."

Plus, from his published interviews: "I want my work to inspire people to really look at nature and feel the emotion I felt when I was in the field." And: "For nature to be healthy, it has to have chaos. Without chaos, it's not in biological order. So as an artist, you visually organize the chaos."

**What works:** "Visually organize the chaos" is the kind of single sentence panels remember. It frames technical skill (composition) as a response to a problem nature poses, not as virtuosity. Conservation is named as a purpose, not a topic.

## 3-4 ANTI-EXAMPLES (do NOT write like this)

### Anti-example A — Third-person curatorial register

> "John Knopf's photographic practice sits at the intersection of the sublime and the technical. Working in the lineage of Ansel Adams, Galen Rowell, and Clyde Butcher, his panoramic landscape work — informed by the Zone System and over twenty years of fieldcraft — interrogates the relationship between place, memory, and light. His images, which have appeared in *National Geographic* and *TIME*, function as meditations on the American West."

**Failures:**
- Third person ("Knopf's practice... his images"). Reads like a gallery wrote it.
- Lineage name-drop in sentence one — borrowing weight rather than earning it.
- "Sits at the intersection of" / "interrogates the relationship between" / "function as meditations on" — three curatorial clichés in one paragraph.
- Three em-dashes in two sentences (LLM tell).
- Opens with WHO the artist is positioned next to, not WHAT the work is after.
- Publication credits inside a meaning sentence — bio material masquerading as statement.

---

### Anti-example B — WHAT-not-WHY opening, gear-list register

> "I shoot panoramic landscape photography using a Phase One IQ4 150MP digital back, large-format film, and graduated ND filters. I have spent the last twenty years photographing the American West — from the slot canyons of Arizona to the granite of the Sierra Nevada to the badlands of South Dakota. My prints are produced using archival pigment processes on cotton rag paper at sizes up to 60 inches."

**Failure modes:**
- Opens with cameras and filters. The panel does not care about your gear in sentence one. They will care later, if at all.
- Locations listed as a travel itinerary ("from… to… to…"). This signals "catalogue of trips," not "sustained body of work."
- Print specs in an artist statement is a category error — that belongs in a price list or a CV.
- Zero stake. Nothing tells the panel what the work is about, only what it is made of.

---

### Anti-example C — LLM-tell em-dash rhythm and abstract abstraction

> "My work explores the liminal space between presence and absence — the way light, refracted through atmosphere, becomes a kind of grammar — a vocabulary of the sublime that the camera can transcribe but never fully translate. I am interested in the moments when landscape ceases to be scenery and becomes, instead, a question — about time, about scale, about the human eye's complicity in what it sees."

**Failure modes:**
- Em-dash every twelve words. This is the single most reliable LLM-prose tell in 2026.
- "Liminal space" / "a kind of grammar" / "vocabulary of the sublime" / "the human eye's complicity" — these are abstractions stacked on abstractions. Nothing in this paragraph could not also be said about a different photographer's work, or a painter's, or a poet's.
- No specific place, no specific subject, no specific project. The reader leaves knowing nothing concrete.
- "Presence and absence" / "liminal" — vocabulary borrowed from academic-fine-art register, which is the wrong register for a commercial-gallery landscape photographer applying to a state arts council.

---

### Anti-example D — Lineage-positioning in the artist's own voice

> "My work descends from the Zone System tradition pioneered by Ansel Adams, refined by Minor White, and carried forward by photographers including Galen Rowell, Clyde Butcher, and QT Luong. Like these masters before me, I approach the landscape with reverence, technical rigor, and a commitment to the long history of black-and-white and color landscape practice in the American West."

**Failure modes:**
- Five names in two sentences. The artist is positioning, not photographing.
- "Like these masters before me" — the artist has just compared himself to five canonical figures. This makes the panel uncomfortable and skeptical.
- Lineage references are appropriate in a *bio* sentence ("trained in the Zone System tradition") or a *cover letter*, never in the artist statement, and never more than 1-2 names total.
- "Reverence, technical rigor, commitment" — three abstract virtues with no specifics behind them.

## VOICE RULES (distilled, prescriptive)

1. **Open with what the artist is after, not with cameras, formats, or a list of locations.** First sentence should land a stake, a question, or a working principle.
2. **First person throughout.** "I shoot…" or "I have spent twenty years…" Never "John Knopf's work…" except in a bio paragraph clearly labeled as bio.
3. **Zero em-dashes (—). Hard rule.** Working artists almost never use em-dashes; em-dash rhythm is the single most reliable LLM-prose tell in 2026. Use commas, periods, parentheses, or colons instead. If the model wants to say "subject — descriptor — close," restructure to two sentences, or use a comma.
4. **Never use the phrases:** "sits at the intersection of," "sits in the lineage of," "interrogates the relationship between," "liminal space," "a kind of grammar," "aesthetic signature," "visual vocabulary," "working grammar," "commercial-gallery register," "meditations on," "informed by."
5. **Technical detail must be justified by what it enables artistically.** Do not list the camera, the format, the filters, or the print process as facts. If the wet plate matters, say what it lets the artist see or do that no other process would.
6. **Lineage references in the artist's own voice: maximum 1-2 names, total, across the whole statement, and only if they are *animating* influences (an idea, a method, a stance you've absorbed) — not positioning markers.** If you find yourself listing three or more names, delete the sentence.
7. **No publication credits inside the statement.** *National Geographic*, *TIME*, etc. belong in the bio paragraph. They are not part of the statement of intent.
8. **Place specificity over place lists.** "I have returned to the same canyon every spring for nine years" beats "I have photographed in Arizona, Utah, Wyoming, Montana, and South Dakota." A list of states reads as a travel log; a return reads as a project.
9. **One sentence in every statement should be quotable.** The "first thought is always of light" / "visually organize the chaos" / "shot or no shot" sentence is what the panel remembers. Engineer for one.
10. **Length 150-300 words for most opportunities.** If the prompt caps at 500, write 280. The panel reads 60+ statements per session; brevity is generosity.
11. **No abstract virtue stacking ("reverence, rigor, commitment").** Replace with one concrete behavior that demonstrates the virtue.
12. **Conservation, civic, or place-stewardship language must be specific to the artist's actual relationship with a place — not added as a value the panel is presumed to want to hear.**

## PER-OPPORTUNITY TAILORING

- **State arts council fellowship (Nevada, NYSCA, Maine, Pennsylvania, NEA Visual Arts):** Lead with the artist's relationship to the state or region. These panels fund *artists committed to working in their state*. Caswell's "my sense of home is tethered to an island off the coast of Maine" is the model. Name the specific landscape, river, range, or city the artist returns to. Civic / conservation framing must be earned by actual ongoing work, not asserted.

- **Landscape / nature photography prize (ILPOTY, OPOTY, NLPA, Critical Mass, Aperture Portfolio Prize):** Lead with project structure, not biography. Panels here want a *body of work*, not greatest hits. Frame as: I have been working on X subject for Y years, returning to Z places, because A. Dobrowner's "I wanted to actively pursue these events" frames Storms as a project, not a portfolio.

- **Photo book / monograph grant (Aperture First Book Award, Anamorphosis, Lucie Foundation Book Prize):** Emphasize sequencing, editorial through-line, book-readiness. Make clear the artist has thought about the work as a book — not a stack of prints. Reference project arc, scope, working title, approximate image count.

- **Museum acquisition / curatorial review:** Emphasize project structure and conceptual through-line. Close the gap between "catalogue of beautiful locations" and "sustained, structured body of work." Curators fund the *project*, not the photographs. Articulate the through-line in a single sentence.

- **General photography prize (Sony World, ILPOTY Open, Critical Mass general):** Lean into the specific category (Open vs Landscape vs Travel vs Wildlife). Read three previous winners' statements in the same category before writing.

- **Regional fellowship or residency:** These panels reward specificity and modesty. Avoid international ambition framing. Match ambition to the scale of the opportunity — a body of work, an exhibition, a regional book.

## Quick verification checklist before sending any statement

- First sentence does not contain a camera, a filter, or a lineage name.
- Em-dash count is ZERO. Not "low." Zero.
- First person throughout, or first-person-after-name-once.
- Lineage names mentioned: 0, 1, or 2. Never 3+.
- One sentence is quotable (5–12 words, present-tense, declarative).
- Word count: 150-300 unless prompt explicitly allows more.
- No phrase from the banned-phrases list in voice rule #4.
- Place specificity: at least one named place AND a duration of engagement with it.

## Additional patterns surfaced during research

### Engineer one quotable single-line thesis per statement

Across all 7 GOOD examples, the strongest sentence is 5–12 words, present-tense, declarative:
- "My first thought is always of light." (Rowell)
- "You visually organize the chaos." (Butcher)
- "The subject is always nature, my main tool is light." (Frye)
- "I wanted to actively pursue these events." (Dobrowner)
- "Art is first created for oneself." (Luong)

Hard requirement: every statement must include one such sentence as the structural anchor. Build the surrounding paragraphs around it. This is the sentence the panel remembers.

### Banned vocabulary (extension to voice rule #4)

These words are saturated in the genre to the point of meaninglessness:
- "vision" / "visionary"
- "journey"
- "passion" / "passionate"
- "explore" / "exploration"
- "capture" (use "photograph", "make", "see" instead)
- "story" / "storytelling" (when used as a generic claim)

### Two genres of artist writing — do not conflate

There are two distinct artist-writing artifacts:
1. **About / bio page** — third-person, biographical, career-marker dense ("Knopf has photographed for 20 years…"). Belongs on the gallery website. NOT what an artist statement is.
2. **Artist statement / project statement / award statement** — first-person, animating, claim-driven. Goes in applications.

The Drafter's `artist_statement` material must be the second genre. The current Drafter output produces the first dressed up as the second — that is the core voice failure. When in doubt, re-read the verbatim Caswell, Burtynsky, and Dobrowner statements above.

### State-fellowship-specific pattern: place + threat

Both Caswell (Maine Arts) and Butcher (NEA Medal) ground their statements in a specific place tied to ecological or civic loss:
- Caswell: a Maine island; landscape "consumed by water and the rising ocean."
- Butcher: the Everglades; conservation "for future generations."

For state arts council fellowship statements, this **place + threat** pattern is structural. If the artist's AKB has conservation/civic intent, surface it through a SPECIFIC place the artist returns to (drawn from `bodies_of_work` or `home_base`), not a generic "the planet is beautiful" line. The specificity is what wins the panel.
