# cover-letter-templates

WHEN TO USE: Loaded into the Package Drafter system prompt for the `cover_letter` material. Supplies five concrete letter shapes keyed to `opp.award.type`: foundation grant, gallery open call, residency, public art commission, and museum donation pitch. The Drafter selects the template matching the opportunity's type, then fills the skeleton using the AKB + opportunity data. Each template specifies addressee, opening move, middle paragraph, close, tone register, and length. Voice rules from `artist-statement-voice.md` apply throughout — the templates here govern shape and register, not sentence-level voice.

---

## 1. Universal mechanics

Across all five templates:

- **One page. Never more.** 250–400 words, three to four paragraphs. A two-page artist cover letter marks the writer as junior ([GYST cover letters for artists](https://www.gyst-ink.com/cover-letters)).
- **Format:** business letter. Top-right: artist address and date. Top-left: recipient name, title, institution, address. Salutation, body, sign-off, typed signature line.
- **Addressee:** always research the specific person. "Dear Selection Committee" is a fallback when the institution routes anonymously (most foundations, most residencies). "Dear Mr./Ms. [Lastname]" only if the letter is directed at a named individual (gallery director, curator, grants officer). "To Whom It May Concern" reads lazy and should be used never.
- **Tone register:** formal-but-direct across all five types. Not casual. Not effusive. The artist is a professional writing to another professional.
- **Close:** one sentence of thanks + one sentence of next-step (enclosed materials, availability for studio visit, etc.) + "Sincerely," + signature.

Voice specifics per template follow.

## 2. Template A — Foundation grant (Guggenheim, Creative Capital, Pollock-Krasner, Joan Mitchell, USA Fellows)

**Addressee.** Named program officer if the foundation publishes one; otherwise "Dear [Foundation] Fellowship Committee" or "Dear Selection Committee." Pollock-Krasner explicitly accepts a cover letter with the application ([Pollock-Krasner How to Apply](https://www.pkf.org/apply/how-to-apply/)); Guggenheim does not request one separately (the Statement of Plans absorbs that function); Creative Capital's Round I uses the project description in lieu of a letter.

**Length.** 300–400 words.

**Shape.**

- **Opening (2 sentences).** State the application in the first sentence: "This letter accompanies my application for the 2025 [Foundation] Fellowship in [discipline]." Second sentence: one-line project thesis (borrowed from the proposal's first beat).
- **Middle (1 paragraph).** Name *why this foundation*. The strongest version cites one or two specific recent recipients whose practice the applicant's work sits adjacent to, with the specific body of work named. "Matthew Brandt's Lakes and Reservoirs (2024 Fellow) operates in the indexical-material register this project extends from a different lineage." The claim is lineage-fit, not imitation. Weak version: "I admire the Foundation's commitment to the arts."
- **Middle, continued (1 short paragraph, optional).** Name the credential most relevant to the proposed project. Not a CV summary — one credential. "A prior ten-year body of work on these same three rivers (*Kennebec River Book*, 2014; *Frozen Harbor*, 2019) establishes the access and working weather competence the project requires."
- **Pollock-Krasner exception.** The P-K cover letter uniquely carries the financial-need argument, stating purpose and amount ([Pollock-Krasner How to Apply](https://www.pkf.org/apply/how-to-apply/)). One paragraph: purpose (studio rent / materials / medical / specific project), amount ($X), and how the funds advance the practice. Do not apologize for asking; state the need flat.
- **Close.** "Enclosed please find the application materials requested: career narrative, statement of plans, list of works, work samples, and three referee contacts. I welcome any questions from the committee."

## 3. Template B — Gallery open call / juried exhibition

**Addressee.** Named gallery director or curator. Gallery cover letters are read one at a time by a named human; "To Whom It May Concern" is disqualifying.

**Length.** 250–350 words.

**Shape.**

- **Opening (2 sentences).** "I write to submit work for consideration in [exhibition title / the gallery's open call for 2025]. The enclosed portfolio presents ten images from *[project title]*, a [medium, year range] body made on [location]." The subject and specificity in sentence two do most of the work.
- **Middle (1 paragraph).** Gallery-fit argument. The strongest version names one artist on the gallery's current roster whose practice sits adjacent to the submitted work. "The serial-return structure places the work in conversation with [gallery artist]'s [specific body]; both use repeated vantage as a climate-witness device." If the gallery roster page reveals no adjacency, the letter is being sent to the wrong gallery — re-target.
- **Middle, continued (1 short paragraph).** Exhibition context. One sentence on recent exhibition history — the single strongest prior show, named by institution. Not a CV summary.
- **Close.** "Print specifications, edition information, and installation dimensions are enclosed with each image. I would welcome a studio visit if useful; I am available [date range] and can bring physical prints to Chelsea."

**Tone note.** Gallery letters skew slightly warmer than foundation letters — a gallery relationship is personal in a way a foundation relationship is not — but the warmth is in specificity, not in effusiveness.

## 4. Template C — Residency (MacDowell, Yaddo, Skowhegan, Headlands, Ucross)

**Addressee.** "Dear [Residency] Admissions Committee" — MacDowell's panels are anonymous and rotate every three years ([MacDowell Application Guidelines](https://www.macdowell.org/apply/application-guidelines)); Yaddo's admissions process is committee-based and references are not required ([Yaddo Apply](https://yaddo.org/apply/)).

**Length.** 250–350 words. Some residencies ask the cover-letter role to be carried by a "project description" field in their application form — in which case this file's content maps into that field.

**Shape.**

- **Opening (2 sentences).** "I am applying for a [season] [year] residency at [name] to complete work on *[project title]*, a [medium, stage] body." The *stage* matters: "begin," "extend," or "complete" all read differently to the panel. Residencies favor projects where the residency period will visibly move the work forward — "extend" and "complete" typically read stronger than "begin."
- **Middle (1 paragraph).** What the residency period specifically enables. This is residency-specific: MacDowell's sustained isolation, Yaddo's cross-disciplinary studio conversation, Skowhegan's summer community. Name the specific affordance — not the generic "time and space to work."
- **Middle, continued (1 short paragraph).** Work-sample framing. "The enclosed eight work samples are from the current body, all made within the past four years per the residency's guidelines [MacDowell rule](https://www.macdowell.org/apply/work-sample-requirements). Two are installation views included to convey scale."
- **Close.** "Thank you for the committee's consideration."

**Tone note.** Residencies read the work samples as primary; the letter is a supporting document. Keep it short. A two-paragraph letter that does nothing but restate the application form is acceptable; a dense argumentative letter is not.

## 5. Template D — Public art commission (city agencies, percent-for-art programs, transit authorities)

**Addressee.** Named public art administrator or "Dear [City / Agency] Public Art Committee."

**Length.** 300–400 words. Often accompanied by a longer design proposal with site-response drawings; the cover letter is the framing document, not the proposal itself.

**Shape.**

- **Opening (2 sentences).** "This letter accompanies my response to [RFP number / call name] for the [site / corridor / station] artwork commission. The proposed work is a [medium, scale] addressing [the site's specific condition: pedestrian volume, transit adjacency, sightlines, historical context]."
- **Middle (1 paragraph).** Site-response logic. Public art panels are trained to read *site response* — has the artist visited, measured, photographed, talked to neighbors? Name one concrete site fact ("the west wall receives direct afternoon light from May through August; the proposed palette is tuned to that condition"). Generic "inspired by the community" language reads as a disqualifying tell that the artist has not been on-site.
- **Middle, continued (1 paragraph).** Relevant prior public-art experience. If none, pivot to: the single strongest analog — a large-scale exhibition, a commission, an outdoor work — that demonstrates the artist can deliver at site-scale. Public-art panels are strongly risk-averse about applicants with no site-scale track record.
- **Fabrication and maintenance note (1–2 sentences).** Name the fabricator or studio; name the maintenance plan in one line (epoxied stainless hardware, annual inspection, 20-year material warranty). This is not optional — public art commissioners fund what they believe will survive five winters.
- **Close.** "The full design submission, fabrication plan, installation diagram, insurance binder, and three professional references accompany this letter."

**Tone note.** More procedural than the other templates. The committee includes architects, engineers, and city agency staff alongside arts professionals — the register splits the difference between artist-to-artist and vendor-to-client.

## 6. Template E — Museum donation / acquisition pitch

**Addressee.** Named curator in the relevant department (photography, contemporary, prints and drawings). Use the most specific curator, not the director; directors route unsolicited letters to the relevant curator and the one-week gap loses momentum.

**Length.** 250–350 words.

**Shape.**

- **Opening (2 sentences).** "I write to propose the donation / acquisition of [work title, year, edition, dimensions] to [museum]'s permanent collection, in the [department]." Second sentence: one-line description of the work, framed in the language of the museum's existing collection.
- **Middle (1 paragraph).** Collection-fit argument, with named precedent. "The work extends the museum's holdings in [existing collection strength — the museum's own collection pages are the source] and sits adjacent to [specific work by another artist in the collection]." A museum curator can verify or reject this claim in 30 seconds; getting it right signals the artist has done the collection-research homework, and getting it wrong disqualifies the pitch.
- **Middle, continued (1 paragraph).** Provenance and condition. One sentence of provenance (exhibition history, prior publication); one sentence of condition (framed / unframed, print date, paper, edition status). Museum curators read condition first.
- **Close.** "I would welcome the opportunity to discuss further, and can arrange for the work to be viewed at [studio address / shipper / gallery] at the department's convenience."

**Tone note.** The museum donation letter is the most formal of the five; curators receive these routinely and distinguish serious pitches from hobbyist ones on tone alone. Keep it flat, factual, and short.

## 7. Cross-template failure modes

- **"I am a passionate artist"** — disqualifying opener across all five templates. Passion is assumed; demonstrating it is the work's job.
- **Autobiography in paragraph one.** The letter opens with the application, not with the artist's origin story. Origin-story belongs in the artist statement only when it is the subject of the work.
- **Over-adjectival close.** "Thank you for this incredible opportunity" is weaker than "Thank you for the committee's consideration." Drop the adjective.
- **Missing addressee-specific fit.** Every template's middle paragraph carries some form of "why this institution." If that paragraph reads generic — could describe any foundation, any gallery, any residency — rewrite. The drafter's Rubric Matcher output should supply the named-precedent material for this paragraph.
