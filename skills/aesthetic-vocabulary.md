# aesthetic-vocabulary

WHEN TO USE: Loaded into the Style Analyst and Rubric Matcher system prompts. Defines the controlled vocabulary these agents must use when describing visual work — composition grammar, light types, subject categories, and named formal lineage. Prevents the agents from inventing soft, unfalsifiable language ("evocative," "powerful," "striking"). Every descriptive claim must resolve to a term in this file or a named precedent.

---

## 1. Named precedents (use these instead of adjectives)

When a portfolio resembles a known body of work, say so by name. Cohort membership is more legible to a juror than a paragraph of adjectives.

- **Ansel Adams** — Sierra Nevada, Yosemite, Half Dome (1920s–1970s). Large-format black-and-white, Zone System tonal control, sanctified-wilderness framing, dramatic sky above human-absent land. The reference point for the "monumental Western sublime" register; also the register most contemporary institutional panels read as *conservative* or *historically closed*.
- **Hiroshi Sugimoto — Seascapes (1980–ongoing)** — 8x10 view camera, exposures up to three hours, horizon line *exactly* bisecting the frame, gelatin-silver printing, repetition as conceptual armature across ~250 global bodies of water. ([Fraenkel Gallery](https://fraenkelgallery.com/portfolios/hiroshi-sugimoto-seascapes); [Public Delivery](https://publicdelivery.org/hiroshi-sugimoto-seascape/)) Use as the reference for *centered axial horizon + durational exposure + conceptual repetition*.
- **Stephen Shore — Uncommon Places (1973–79, pub. 1982)** — Tripod-mounted 8x10, banal American vernacular (diners, intersections, motel rooms) rendered in saturated but naturalistic color, deliberately uncomposed-looking but precisely composed. Anchor of the color-photography-as-art canon; participated in the 1975 *New Topographics* exhibition at George Eastman House. ([Vancouver Art Gallery](https://www.vanartgallery.bc.ca/exhibitions/stephen-shore-uncommon-places/); [Aaron Schuman interview](https://www.aaronschuman.com/shoreinterview.html)) Use for *deadpan color vernacular*.
- **William Eggleston — *William Eggleston's Guide* (MoMA, 1976)** — Dye-transfer color, snapshot-adjacent framing but rigorous underlying structure (Szarkowski's introduction notes Eggleston's use of the Confederate-flag X-diagonal as a compositional armature). ([Whitney](https://whitney.org/exhibitions/william-eggleston); [Artforum: Democracy & Chaos](https://www.artforum.com/features/william-eggleston-democracy-chaos-206154/)) Use for *democratic-subject color* — when everything in the frame is treated as equally worth looking at.
- **Bernd & Hilla Becher — Typologies (1960s–2000s)** — Industrial structures (water towers, coal tipples, blast furnaces) photographed frontally from a fixed vantage, on overcast days to eliminate shadow, presented in grids. Founded the Düsseldorf School (Gursky, Ruff, Höfer, Struth). ([Tate](https://www.tate.org.uk/art/artists/bernd-becher-and-hilla-becher-718/who-are-bechers)) Use for *serial typology + overcast-as-subject + flattened frontality*.
- **Richard Misrach — Desert Cantos (1979–ongoing)** — Large-format color, 40+ loosely linked sub-series on the American West; picturesque vistas tuned into indictments (nuclear test sites, Salton Sea, petrochemical corridors). ([Fraenkel Gallery](https://fraenkelgallery.com/portfolios/richard-misrach-desert-cantos); [Pace](https://www.pacegallery.com/artists/richard-misrach/)) Use for *saturated-color critique landscape* — beauty as the delivery vehicle for political content.
- **Lewis Baltz — The New Industrial Parks near Irvine, California (1973–75)** — Deadpan black-and-white of tract industrial architecture, rectilinear compositions built from garage doors, drainage pipes, parking-lot lines. New Topographics core text. ([Frieze](https://www.frieze.com/article/lewis-baltz); [Zander Galerie](https://zandergalerie.com/exhibitions/the-new-industrial-parks-near-irvine-california-the-prototype-works-a52e5393/)) Use for *minimal deadpan vernacular*.
- **Sally Mann — Deep South (1998–2005)** — Wet-plate collodion 19th-century process, Civil-War-era technique applied to Southern landscape, chemical drips and edge failures left in as subject matter. ([Getty: *A Thousand Crossings*](https://www.getty.edu/art/exhibitions/sally_mann/inner.html); [Art Institute of Chicago](https://www.artic.edu/artworks/154289/deep-south-untitled-three-drips)) Use for *process-forward historical-material photography*.
- **Cheryle St. Onge — *Calling the Birds Home* (2020–22)** — 8x10 B&W portraits made alongside her mother through vascular dementia; soft New Hampshire light, intimate scale, refusal of drama. ([LensCratch interview](https://lenscratch.com/2022/09/memoryloss-series-cheryle-st-onge/); [L'Artiere](https://www.lartiere.com/en/prodotto/calling-the-birds-home-cheryle-st-onge/)) Use for *domestic-scale durational document*.
- **Chris McCaw — Sunburn (2003–ongoing)** — Hours-long exposures in which the sun physically scorches the paper negative; solarization reverses tonality; burn holes *are* the image. ([Chris McCaw](https://www.chrismccaw.com/about-sunburn); [SFO Museum](https://www.sfomuseum.org/exhibitions/chris-mccaw-sunburn); [Met](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/289113)) Use for *cameraless / material-trace photography*.
- **Matthew Brandt — Dust, Lakes and Reservoirs (2008–ongoing)** — Gum-bichromate prints where the pigment is dust from a demolished building or water from the photographed lake. ([Yossi Milo](https://yossimilo.com/artists/46-matthew-brandt/biography/); [Interview Magazine](https://www.interviewmagazine.com/art/matthew-brandt)) Use for *photography-as-material-indexing*.

**Anti-references (name these only to mark a portfolio as outside institutional register):**

- **Peter Lik** — commercial-gallery HDR, over-saturated sunbursts, composite moons sold as documentary; a critic in the PetaPixel coverage called the work "kitsch" with "not the slightest artistic value." ([PetaPixel](https://petapixel.com/2018/02/06/peter-lik-called-photographers-faked-moon-photo/); [Fstoppers](https://fstoppers.com/critiques/tale-two-moons-peter-liks-photographs-called-out-science-218194)) The stylistic signal: dynamic-range exaggeration, sunstars, magenta-to-teal preset skies, mall-gallery scale.
- **Trey Ratcliff** — tone-mapped HDR travel photography, high-chroma preset stack; useful for naming the HDR-tourist register that institutional panels treat as disqualifying.

## 2. Composition grammar

Use these terms, not "well-composed" / "balanced" / "strong."

- **Centered axial** — dominant subject or horizon on the central vertical or horizontal axis (Sugimoto, the Bechers). Institutional when paired with conceptual seriality; weak when decorative.
- **Rule-of-thirds horizon** — horizon on upper or lower third. The *default* output of consumer cameras and presets; its presence across an entire portfolio without variation is a weak signal.
- **Foreground repoussoir** — near-edge element (rock, branch) guiding the eye inward; a classical-landscape device (Claude Lorrain → Adams). Reads as conservative when unexamined.
- **Deep space vs flattened space** — receding atmospheric perspective vs compressed picture plane (the Düsseldorf school flattens; Misrach often goes deep). Note which is chosen and whether choice is consistent.
- **Frontal / orthogonal address** — the subject parallel to the film plane, no rake (Bechers, Baltz). Reads as typological; requires serial context to land.
- **Panoramic crop** — 16:9, 2:1, or wider. Default output of iPhone pano mode; as a *default* it reads weak. As a *decision* (consistent across body, tied to content) it reads strong.
- **Square** — Rolleiflex/Hasselblad format; Instagram-adjacent. Legibility depends on whether squareness is load-bearing (Sally Mann, Nicholas Nixon's *Brown Sisters*) or merely convenient.
- **Vertical landscape** — a deliberate choice that signals resistance to the horizontal default.
- **Stitched / composite frames** — when declared and conceptual, institutional; when hidden to fake dynamic range, disqualifying.

## 3. Light types (name the condition, not the mood)

- **Golden hour** — low-angle warm light, ~30–60 min after sunrise / before sunset. Overuse across an entire portfolio is a weak signal (preset-driven).
- **Blue hour / civil twilight** — sun 0–6° below horizon, ambient cyan-to-magenta gradient. A technical capability marker when clean; a preset marker when every frame has the same gradient.
- **Overcast-as-subject** — diffuse, shadowless, chromatically neutral. The Bechers' chosen condition; the Düsseldorf default. Reads *institutional* when paired with frontality.
- **Direct midday** — hard top light, short shadows. Avoided by tourist-landscape photographers, embraced by the New Topographics cohort.
- **God-rays / crepuscular rays** — beams through cloud or foliage. A commercial-register marker unless the image is otherwise unsentimental.
- **Long-exposure water smoothing** — >1s, water rendered as mist. A capability marker on first appearance; a cliché when applied indiscriminately.
- **Long-exposure sky** — star trails, cloud blur, or (McCaw) sun burn-through.
- **HDR tone-mapping** — compressed dynamic range with local-contrast halos. A weak signal unless deployed deliberately (Gursky's *99 Cent*).
- **Night / available-dark** — high ISO, existing-light interiors; a separate lineage (Bill Henson, Todd Hido).
- **Flash / on-camera strobe** — a declaration; flags a Diane-Arbus / Mark-Cohen lineage rather than a landscape lineage.

## 4. Palette vocabulary

- **Muted earth tones** — desaturated ochres, umbers, greys. Stephen Shore, Joel Sternfeld, Alec Soth register.
- **Saturated natural chroma** — Misrach / Eggleston; color *is* the content, but within the physically plausible.
- **HDR-violet-gold** — the Peter Lik / Trey Ratcliff preset signature; sunset skies pushed magenta-to-amber, shadows lifted past plausibility. **Disqualifying for institutional framing.**
- **Cyan-magenta preset signature** — the "orange-and-teal" cinematic LUT applied indiscriminately; legible in under one second to a panelist.
- **Monochrome silver-gelatin register** — the tonal vocabulary of printed B&W work; Zone-System-aware shadow detail, paper-white highlights, neutral blacks.
- **Split-tone / duotone** — sepia + cool highlight, selenium blue, platinum warm. A printing-tradition marker.
- **Complementary orange-sun / cyan-water** — golden-hour coastal frames; physical, but overused.
- **Color as subject** — Eggleston's democratic-camera mode: the palette *is* the work (the red ceiling, the green bathroom).

## 5. Formal lineage phrasing

Prefer these constructions to adjective piles:

- "Sits in the [New Topographics / Düsseldorf School / f/64 / Pictorialist / Boston School] lineage."
- "Cohort: [Soth, Sternfeld, Sternfeld-adjacent / Sugimoto-adjacent durational / Bechers-adjacent typological]."
- "A [Misrach-register / Shore-register / Adams-register] landscape."
- "Process-forward in the [wet-plate / cyanotype / gum-bichromate / cameraless] tradition."
- "Reads as [institutional / commercial-gallery / regional-camera-club]."

Do not write "reminiscent of" or "in the style of." Write *adjacent to* or *lineage of* and name the work.

## 6. Weak-signal markers (call these out explicitly)

- **Preset-driven consistency** — color grading more uniform than the depicted scenes could physically be.
- **Range gap** — 30 frames of sunrise in one national park. Signals vacation-based practice, not sustained project.
- **Technical hesitation** — tripod holes visible in group A, handheld blur in group B, no pattern linking the two.
- **Commercial register crossover** — watermarks, print-for-sale sizing callouts, "available as metal" captions.
- **Unexamined repoussoir** — every frame has a branch in the lower-left corner.
- **HDR halos** — ghosting around high-contrast edges.
- **Ceremonial titling** — "Majesty," "Eternal," "Awakening." Reads commercial.
- **Caption-as-explanation** — work that needs a paragraph of location-and-equipment to function.

## 7. Painting and sculpture (concise, for mixed-medium applicants)

**Painting formal vocabulary** (adapted from the [Merriam-Webster art-terms glossary](https://www.merriam-webster.com/wordplay/art-terms-techniques-vocabulary), [Gamblin](https://gamblincolors.com/painting-glossary/), [Smithsonian Museum Conservation Institute](https://mci.si.edu/painting-conservation-glossary-terms)):

- **Chiaroscuro** — modeled light/shadow producing volumetric depth (Caravaggio, Leonardo).
- **Sfumato** — smoky tonal transitions, no visible boundary.
- **Impasto** — thick paint ridged above the picture plane (van Gogh, Auerbach).
- **Glaze** — translucent layer modifying the color beneath.
- **Scumble** — dry, broken, semi-opaque over-layer that softens.
- **Ground / imprimatura** — tinted underlayer visible through thin passages.
- **Alla prima** — completed in a single wet session.
- **Grisaille** — monochrome underpainting or finished work.

**Sculpture formal vocabulary:**

- **Truth to materials** — Brâncuși / Moore lineage; material dictates form.
- **Assemblage / accumulation** — Nevelson, Arman.
- **Cast vs direct** — whether the object was modeled and cast (lost-wax, bronze) or carved/welded directly.
- **Base / pedestal / plinth handling** — pedestal-integral (Brâncuși) vs pedestal-refused (Serra, floor works).
- **Scale register** — hand (jewelry, cabinet), body (bust, full figure), architectural (Serra, Heizer).
- **Patina** — chemical or applied surface; a deliberate palette.

## 8. How the Rubric Matcher must use this file

When describing any portfolio, every formal claim must either (a) cite a term from sections 2–4, or (b) name a precedent from section 1. Do not pass work through unannotated. If a portfolio's closest lineage is a section-1 anti-reference (Lik, Ratcliff), say so — that is the most useful signal a matcher can return, because it predicts institutional rejection more reliably than any positive signal predicts acceptance.
