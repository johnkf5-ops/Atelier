# artist-statement-voice

WHEN TO USE: Loaded into the Package Drafter system prompt whenever the material being generated is an `artist_statement` or the opening paragraph of a `cover_letter`. Defines the voice, grammar, and anti-pattern list that separates an institutional-register statement from MFA-workshop filler. This is a voice file, not a content file — medium-specific lineage references live in `artist-statement-voice-by-medium.md`.

---

## 1. The structural rule

A strong institutional artist statement is three paragraphs or fewer, ~200–350 words, and resolves in this order:

1. **What the work IS** — medium, subject, method, scale. Concrete nouns. No throat-clearing.
2. **What the work DOES formally** — the compositional / material / procedural decisions and what they produce.
3. **Where the work sits** — lineage, conversation, current body. One sentence. No more.

Jurors read the first sentence and the last sentence with the most attention and skim the middle. Design for that reading pattern: the first sentence must be the thesis of the body of work, stated flat. The last sentence must situate the work without overclaiming. Everything in between earns its place or gets cut.

Length varies by program. The Guggenheim Statement of Plans caps at 1,000 words ([Guggenheim Application Guide, 2026 cycle](https://competition.gf.org/assets/Guide-to-the-JSGMF-2026.pdf)); the Joan Mitchell Fellowship asks for three 300-word statements plus a 150-word biography ([Joan Mitchell Fellowship Application Guidelines](https://www.joanmitchellfoundation.org/artist-programs/joan-mitchell-fellowship-application-guidelines-2025)); MacDowell's work-sample `additional details` boxes absorb one to three sentences per image ([MacDowell Work Sample Requirements](https://www.macdowell.org/apply/work-sample-requirements)). All three reward compression. A 900-word Guggenheim statement that reads like a 350-word statement with three appendices beats a tight 900-word statement where every sentence is load-bearing — because jurors stop reading.

## 2. Anti-patterns (banned verbs and adjectives)

These are the default MFA-workshop filler that marks a statement as generic within one sentence. The ban is absolute in the first and last paragraphs; the middle may use one of these only when the verb is load-bearing and no concrete substitute exists.

- **Verbs to cut:** *explores, examines, interrogates, investigates, questions, probes, delves, unpacks, grapples, negotiates, navigates, inhabits, confronts, blurs, challenges, disrupts, re-imagines, re-envisions, bridges, weaves, transcends.* If the statement leads with one of these, the panelist has already seen this statement 40 times this cycle.
- **Adjectives to cut:** *haunting, evocative, powerful, striking, luminous, ethereal, visceral, liminal, poetic, lyrical, meditative, complex, nuanced, multifaceted, layered, rich, deep, profound, timeless, universal.* These are feelings the work may produce in the viewer; they are not descriptions of the work.
- **Phrases to cut:** *"the human condition," "the intersection of X and Y," "in our increasingly Z world," "what it means to be," "a dialogue between," "the tension between," "a sense of," "a meditation on."*
- **Abstract-noun openings:** *"Memory," "Time," "Place," "Identity," "Loss"* as a one-word or dropped-in opening. Panelists treat these as filler instantly.

Replace every banned term with a concrete noun, a verb of physical action (prints, stacks, floods, drips, crops, exposes, paints over, cuts, pours), or a named formal decision.

---

## 3. Voice rules

- **Third person, present tense.** "Brandt prints his Lakes series by submerging the exposed paper in the water of the photographed lake." Not: "I explore the relationship between water and image." Third-person reads as institutional; first-person reads as MFA thesis — unless the program's own guidelines prescribe first-person (many residencies do; follow the prescription).
- **Concrete over abstract.** Name the actual river, the actual year, the actual camera, the actual paper, the actual chemical. "Platinum-palladium prints on Arches Platine" beats "hand-crafted archival prints."
- **Proper nouns as lineage markers, not as name-dropping.** If the work sits adjacent to Eggleston or Sugimoto or the Bechers, name them — in passing, with the specific body of work, and only if the adjacency is load-bearing. See `artist-statement-voice-by-medium.md` for per-medium precedent lists.
- **No biography in the statement unless the biography is the subject.** Tarrah Krajnak's Peru-born-transracial-adoptee history sits in her statement because the work *is* about it ([1854 Photography profile](https://www.1854.photography/2024/03/tarrah-krajnaks-marginal-meandering-histories/)). Most artists have no equivalent. Save biography for the career narrative.
- **One thesis sentence, not three.** If three sentences sound like they are each trying to be the thesis, two of them are there because the writer was unsure which was strongest. Pick one.

## 4. Worked example — strong (photography, mid-career landscape in Guggenheim register)

### Body of work: *River Ice, 2021–2024*

> Knopf photographs the annual breakup of river ice on the Kennebec, Androscoggin, and Penobscot in Maine, working in 4x5 color negative and printing at 40x50 inches on matte cotton rag. The frames are made from the same three or four vantage points year over year, at the exact hour the ice begins to move. The serial structure produces a flattened picture plane and a tight palette — slate, rust, lichen green — that foregrounds the physical record of a warming winter.
>
> The series extends a decade of project-based landscape practice rooted in the New Topographics lineage (Shore, Baltz) and the durational-return structure of Sugimoto's *Seascapes* ([Fraenkel Gallery](https://fraenkelgallery.com/portfolios/hiroshi-sugimoto-seascapes)). It is in conversation with Cheryle St. Onge's *Calling the Birds Home* as a model of domestic-scale durational document ([L'Artiere](https://www.lartiere.com/en/prodotto/calling-the-birds-home-cheryle-st-onge/)).
>
> A first selection of twenty-four images will be exhibited at Dowling Walsh in 2025; a book is in development with TIS Books. The river-ice work is the present axis of a practice that will next extend to the estuarine corridor where these rivers meet the Gulf of Maine.

**Why this works.** Sentence one names four concrete facts (rivers, format, scale, paper). Sentence two names the formal decisions (repeated vantage, serial structure, palette) and what they produce. The second paragraph places the work in lineage *by named precedent*, not by adjective. The third paragraph closes with concrete near-future deliverables that the rest of the application (career narrative, CV, work samples) can verify.

## 5. Worked example — weak (same material, default register)

> My work explores the complex, haunting relationship between humans and the natural world in an age of climate change. Through my large-format photographs of rivers, I investigate the tension between permanence and loss, creating evocative images that invite viewers into a meditative dialogue with the landscape. My practice is informed by the rich tradition of American landscape photography, but pushes beyond it to interrogate what it means to witness in the Anthropocene.

**Why this fails.** Four banned verbs (explores, investigates, invite, interrogate) in three sentences; six banned adjectives (complex, haunting, evocative, meditative, rich, pushes beyond); three banned phrases (the tension between, a dialogue with, what it means to). Zero concrete nouns. A panelist reading 400 of these cannot remember it 30 seconds later. The work could be any landscape project from any artist; the statement has erased its subject.

## 6. Worked example — strong (cover letter opening)

> St. Onge's *Calling the Birds Home* — 8x10 portraits of her mother made alongside the progression of vascular dementia — is the model for the proposed work, and the Foundation's recent support of care-as-subject photography in its 2022 and 2023 cycles is the reason the application is directed here rather than to a documentary-photojournalism program.

**Why this works.** One sentence. Names a specific precedent and a specific cohort pattern. Explains why *this* institution, not a generic one. The drafter can re-use the shape — named precedent + named cohort pattern + reason for this application — for any opportunity whose recent cycles the Rubric Matcher has analyzed.

## 7. Worked example — strong (painting, mid-career in Pollock-Krasner register)

> The paintings in *Afterimage* are oil on un-stretched linen, 60 x 48 inches, hung from the top edge with a one-inch reveal. They begin as a flat ground of lead-tin yellow under-painting, are worked wet-into-wet across a single session, and are finished with a scumbled grey that lifts the ochre in broken patches. The method is alla prima adapted from the working practice Luc Tuymans describes as one painting per session, conceived in advance ([Apollo Magazine interview](https://apollo-magazine.com/a-necessary-realism-interview-with-luc-tuymans/)), and the scale sits inside the figure-ground register of Marlene Dumas's *Great Men* portraits rather than the picture-plane-dominant register of large-format abstraction.

**Why this works.** Material, dimensions, mount, and procedure in the first two sentences; two named precedents with specific works in the third; zero banned verbs; zero adjectives of effect. A juror who knows Tuymans and Dumas will be able to picture the work before reaching the end of the paragraph.

## 8. The final-read pass

Before shipping a statement, run four checks:

1. **Replace test.** Could this statement describe a different artist's work if three proper nouns were swapped out? If yes, it is filler. Rewrite.
2. **Adjective budget.** How many adjectives in 300 words? More than ten, cut.
3. **First-sentence test.** Read only sentence one. Does a panelist know what the work looks like? If no, rewrite.
4. **Last-sentence test.** Does the final sentence land the work somewhere specific — a next body, a deliverable, a lineage — or drift into generic aspiration? Generic aspiration is a cut.

A statement that passes these four checks is not automatically strong. But a statement that fails any of them is reliably weak, and rewriting is cheaper than losing the application to it.
